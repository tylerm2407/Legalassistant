# ADR 005 — Action generator scope

**Date:** 2026-03-27
**Status:** Accepted
**Deciders:** CaseMate founding team

---

## Decision

CaseMate generates three types of action documents after any legal conversation: a demand letter, a rights summary, and a next-steps checklist. All three are pre-filled from the user's profile, generated by Claude, and downloadable. We do not generate court filings, contracts, or complex legal documents at launch.

---

## Context

Legal guidance has limited value if it ends with "now you know your rights." The gap between knowing your rights and acting on them is where most people get stuck. They know their landlord is wrong. They don't know what to say or write.

The action generator closes that gap. After a conversation about a deposit dispute, the user can generate a formal demand letter that cites the correct statute, names the specific violation, states the remedy they are entitled to, and sets a deadline — all pre-filled with their actual details, ready to send today.

This is the feature that makes the product feel real. It is the moment in the demo where judges go quiet.

---

## The three actions

### 1. Demand letter

A formal letter from the user to the opposing party. Used for:
- Landlord deposit disputes
- Debt collector violations (FDCPA cease and desist)
- Employer wage theft recovery
- Warranty or consumer protection claims
- Contract breach notification

Structure:
```
[User name and address]
[Date]
[Recipient name and address]

Re: [Issue summary]

Dear [Recipient],

[Opening — states the situation and the user's relationship to the recipient]

[Body — states the specific violation with statute citation]

[Remedy — states exactly what the user is requesting and by when]

[Consequence — states what the user will do if the demand is not met]

[Closing]

[User name]
```

All fields pre-filled from the user's profile and the conversation context. The user reviews, adjusts the tone if desired, and sends.

### 2. Rights summary

A plain-English one-page document explaining the user's specific rights in their situation. Used when:
- The user wants to understand their position before a negotiation
- They need to share their rights with someone else (family member, employer)
- They want a reference document to keep

Structure:
- What the law says (in plain English, no legalese)
- What you are specifically entitled to (calculated amounts where applicable)
- What the other party is required to do
- What happens if they don't comply
- Where to go for more help (state AG office, small claims court, legal aid)

### 3. Next-steps checklist

A prioritized action list with deadlines. Used when:
- The situation requires multiple steps over time
- There are documentation requirements (photographing damage, sending certified mail)
- There are hard deadlines (statute of limitations, response windows)

Structure:
- Immediate actions (today / this week)
- Documentation to gather
- Letters to send (with deadlines)
- Government agencies to contact
- Court options if resolution fails
- Estimated timeline and cost

## Implementation

### Generation prompt pattern

All three actions use the same underlying pattern: inject the user's full profile and the conversation context, then ask Claude to generate the specific document.

```python
# backend/actions/letter_generator.py

def build_letter_prompt(profile: LegalProfile, conversation: list[dict]) -> str:
    """
    Builds the prompt for generating a demand letter.

    Injects the user's full legal profile and conversation context so the
    letter is specific to their situation — not a generic template with
    blank fields to fill in.

    The generated letter is complete and ready to send after user review.
    It cites the specific statute for the user's state, names the exact
    violation, and calculates the specific remedy owed.

    Args:
        profile: User's complete legal profile from Supabase.
        conversation: The conversation that prompted the letter request.

    Returns:
        A complete system + user prompt string for the letter generation call.
    """
```

### What makes the letters good

The key difference between CaseMate's demand letters and a generic template:

**Generic template:**
> "I am writing to demand the return of my security deposit of $______. You are required by law to return it within __ days."

**CaseMate demand letter:**
> "I am writing to demand the return of my security deposit of $2,200, plus interest accrued since January 2024, pursuant to Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 186, Section 15B. As you failed to conduct a move-in inspection within 10 days of the commencement of my tenancy as required by M.G.L. c.186 §15B(2), you have forfeited the right to make any deduction from my security deposit for damages. You are required to return the full deposit plus interest within 30 days of the termination of my tenancy. Failure to comply entitles me to recover three times the deposit amount plus reasonable attorney fees under M.G.L. c.186 §15B(7)."

The difference is the user's actual state, the correct statute section, the calculated amount, the specific violation (missing inspection), and the specific remedy including the 3x multiplier. None of that comes from a template — it comes from the profile and state law library.

## Scope boundaries — what we do NOT generate at launch

**Court filings:** Small claims complaints, motions, formal pleadings. These require precise procedural formatting that varies by court and jurisdiction. A filing error can harm the user's case. Out of scope until we can verify accuracy by jurisdiction.

**Contracts:** Employment agreements, lease agreements, contractor agreements. Generating contracts requires understanding the full negotiation context and carries significant liability risk if terms are unclear or unenforceable. Out of scope.

**Wills and estate documents:** High stakes, highly jurisdiction-specific, require notarization and witnesses. Out of scope.

**Immigration filings:** USCIS forms, petitions, asylum applications. Errors have severe consequences including deportation. CaseMate provides immigration information but does not generate filings.

The demand letter, rights summary, and checklist cover the highest-frequency actions users actually need and carry manageable risk. The letter is asserting a right, not initiating litigation. The user reviews it before sending.

## Alternatives considered

**Only generate demand letters (single action type)**
Rejected. The rights summary serves a different use case (understanding and sharing) and the checklist serves cases where no single letter resolves the situation. Three types covers significantly more user scenarios than one.

**Full template library with user fill-in-the-blank**
Rejected. Templates with blank fields are not meaningfully better than what users can find on Google. The value is in pre-filling from the profile with correct legal citations — not in the template structure itself.

**Generate court filings at launch**
Rejected. The precision required and the consequences of errors make court filing generation too high-risk for an MVP. Adding it requires jurisdiction-specific filing format verification that cannot be done in 36 hours.

**Allow users to choose tone (formal/firm/aggressive)**
Accepted as a future feature. For MVP, all letters default to firm-but-professional. Tone customization will be added after the core generation quality is validated.

---

## Consequences

**Positive:**
- Closes the gap between legal knowledge and legal action
- The letter generation is the single most demo-able feature — judges can see a complete, ready-to-send legal letter appear in seconds
- Pre-filling from profile means the output is immediately usable, not a template to complete
- Covers the three highest-frequency user actions without the liability risk of court filings

**Negative:**
- Letter quality depends on profile completeness — sparse profiles produce generic letters
- Users may send letters without reviewing them, potentially creating issues if context was misunderstood
- Cannot cover every possible legal document type — scope must be clearly communicated to users

---

## User guidance

Every generated document includes:
- A header noting CaseMate is not a licensed attorney and the document should be reviewed before sending
- A recommendation to consult a licensed attorney for litigation or complex situations
- Links to relevant state resources (AG office, legal aid, court self-help centers)

This protects users from over-relying on the document and protects CaseMate from unauthorized practice of law claims.
